\begin{abstract}
This paper is not submission-ready for ICLR main. The original research bet was that a robot can predict micro-slip before stable contact is visually apparent and use that prediction to improve grasp control. We rebuilt the repository around a deterministic tactile/precontact mechanics benchmark with four grasp tasks, five distribution shifts, seven seeds, nine predictors/controllers, seven ablations, and a combined stress sweep. The evidence is mixed but terminally negative. On the combined hard shift, the proposed precontact model improves early-warning recall over a vision threshold by 0.571 and improves grasp success by 0.184, but it loses average precision to the strongest non-oracle baseline: paired AP difference -0.016 with a 95 percent CI of 0.018. It also has high false-alarm rate (0.817) and high control cost (0.929). Ablations contradict the mechanism: vision-only precontact reaches AP 0.766 and minus-friction reaches AP 0.764, both above the full method's AP 0.744. The honest terminal decision is \textbf{KILL/ARCHIVE}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Question}
The draft asked whether a robot can forecast micro-slip before stable contact and adjust grasp control earlier than conventional tactile or force-threshold systems. This is a crowded tactile robotics question. Prior work already includes tactile-driven slip prediction, sensor-less fingertip contact and force estimation, soft barometric and capacitive tactile sensors, distributed contact-force slip detection, friction estimation before gross slip, and high-resolution optical tactile systems.

The rebuild therefore uses a hostile gate: precontact micro-slip prediction must beat strong tactile, physics, and uncertainty baselines on both prediction quality and closed-loop grasp outcomes. Early lead time alone is not enough.

\section{Benchmark}
Each episode simulates a gripper approaching an object with hidden friction, compliance, surface texture, approach angle, actuator lag, visual occlusion, and sensor noise. The benchmark covers four tasks: smooth low-friction picking, textured fragile picking, curved-surface pinching, and compliant-object lifting. The distribution shifts are nominal precontact, low-friction shift, compliance shift, sensor-noise shift, and combined hard shift.

The main run contains 120,960 episode-level rollouts. The ablation run contains 18,816 rollouts. The stress sweep contains 56,448 rollouts. The evidence was re-run from source on 2026-06-15 and is regenerated with:

python src\run_experiment.py
\end{verbatim}

\section{Compared Methods}
The compared methods are a vision gap threshold, normal-force threshold, force-derivative detector, friction-cone margin estimator, tactile temporal filter, optical-tactile classifier, ensemble uncertainty guard, the proposed precontact micro-slip model, and an oracle precontact risk model. The proposed method uses precontact shear, friction prior, compliance estimate, approach geometry, latency margin, and sensor noise to predict future micro-slip and trigger preemptive tightening or slowing.

\section{Closed-Loop Control}
The proposed model reaches 0.226 grasp success and 0.773 gross-slip rate on combined hard shift. This is much better than vision-only thresholding, but it is roughly tied with the friction-cone margin and below the ensemble uncertainty guard. More importantly, it achieves the gain by triggering many interventions: false-alarm rate 0.817 and control cost 0.929. That is too brittle for a main-conference robotics claim.

\section{Ablation Failure}
Table~\ref{tab:ablations} is the strongest reason to archive the paper. The full precontact mechanism does not dominate its own stripped variants. Vision-only precontact reaches AP 0.766 and minus-friction reaches AP 0.764, both above full precontact AP 0.744. The compliance and friction terms appear to add false positives under the simulated shifts rather than a robust mechanism.

\section{Stress Sweep}
At maximum stress, the proposed method reaches AP 0.761 and success 0.207. The optical-tactile baseline reaches AP 0.769, and the ensemble guard reaches higher early recall and success. The stress gate therefore fails: precontact modeling remains plausible, but not decisive.

\section{Decision}
The rebuilt evidence gives a useful negative result. Precontact warning can increase early recall and improve control relative to weak visual thresholds, but this paper cannot honestly claim ICLR-main readiness. It lacks hardware or accepted high-fidelity tactile validation, fails the AP gate, carries excessive false alarms/control cost, and is contradicted by ablations. The terminal recommendation is \textbf{KILL/ARCHIVE}. Revival requires a real tactile dataset or simulator, stronger calibration, and a mechanism that beats friction-cone, optical-tactile, and uncertainty baselines without simply over-triggering.
